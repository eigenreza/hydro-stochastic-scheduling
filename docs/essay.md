# Where Flexibility Value Lives, and When Estimating It Stops Being Meaningful: Evidence from a Norwegian Hydropower Cascade

*Draft for internal review. Not submitted anywhere. All figures and numbers below are computed directly from the backtest outputs in this repository (`results/tables/`, `data/processed/`); none are approximated or invented for this document.*

## Abstract

We quantify the value of operational flexibility in weekly hydropower reservoir scheduling using 22 years (2003-2024) of real inflow and electricity price data from the Arendalsvassdraget system in southern Norway, comparing an open-loop policy committed in advance, a closed-loop policy that adapts quarterly, and a perfect-foresight benchmark. Using a three-plant cascade model resolving Jorundland, Evenstad, and Rygene individually, we show two results. First, the value of flexibility concentrates entirely at the single node with real reservoir storage and is exactly zero at the two run-of-river plants, a result we establish analytically rather than only empirically. Second, naive estimates of flexibility value become statistically unreliable once realized prices move outside the distribution the forecasting model was calibrated on, and we trace this to a specific, measurable mechanism: a reversal of the historical seasonal price pattern during the 2021-2022 energy crisis. Restricting to years without such a shock, a small positive flexibility premium of approximately 3.4 MNOK per year is statistically detectable at the 1 percent level.

## 1. Introduction

Operators of hydropower reservoirs face a recurring decision under two layers of uncertainty: how much water will arrive in the coming weeks and months, and what electricity will be worth when that water is eventually released. A natural question for operators, investors, and regulators alike is how much it is actually worth to keep a release plan flexible rather than committing to one months in advance, and the answer bears on reservoir capacity investment, the design of forecasting and scheduling tools, and when simplified or aggregated models of hydropower systems can be trusted.

This question has a long history in the stochastic programming literature applied to energy systems, with a well-established methodological answer in multistage stochastic programming and, for hydropower specifically, stochastic dual dynamic programming (SDDP). Less well documented, at least grounded in real multi-decade data for a specific real system, is where exactly the value of flexibility lives inside a real cascade of plants, and what happens to estimates of that value when the market conditions used to calibrate the forecasting model are violated by reality.

This paper makes two contributions, using the Arendalsvassdraget cascade in southern Norway as a real test system with 22 years of real historical inflow and price data.

**Contribution 1.** In a real three-plant cascade, the value of flexibility concentrates entirely at the node with physical reservoir storage, and is exactly, not approximately, zero at nodes without it, regardless of how the release policy is implemented. We demonstrate this analytically, not just empirically, for the Arendalsvassdraget system.

**Contribution 2.** Estimates of the value of flexibility become unstable, in a specific and traceable way, when the realized market regime departs from the historical distribution used to calibrate the forecasting model. We show this is not a property of the optimization method or an unexplained statistical artifact, but is attributable to a measurable mismatch between the historical seasonal price shape and the realized shape during the 2021-2022 European energy crisis, and we quantify how strongly this instability scales with the severity of the price shock.

As a secondary result, once years affected by the crisis are excluded, the value of flexibility at the storage node is small but statistically significant and positive, giving a clean answer to the original motivating question under ordinary market conditions.

## 2. Related work

The general framework used here, comparing a policy that commits to decisions before uncertainty resolves against one that adapts as information arrives, follows the structure of multi-horizon stochastic programming used in capacity expansion and energy systems planning, as in the EMPIRE model developed at NTNU and SINTEF (Tomasgard, Crespo del Granado, and collaborators). For medium-term hydropower scheduling specifically, the established state-of-the-art approach is stochastic dual dynamic programming, used in operational tools such as SHOP and in the broader Norwegian hydropower scheduling literature associated with Helseth, Mo, and Fleten. The present work implements a tractable multistage stochastic program sharing SDDP's conceptual structure, non-anticipativity, recourse, stage-wise decomposition, but is not SDDP itself, so the closed-loop results here should be read as a lower bound on what a full SDDP implementation would recover, not as an estimate of it. *The citations in this paragraph name real researchers and research groups identified during earlier work on this project, but specific paper titles, years, and venues have not been independently re-verified for this draft and would need a citation check before any submission.*

## 3. Data and methods

**Data.** The dataset covers 2003-2024 (22 backtest years, with hydrological and price records extending back to 2000 for calibration). Inflow is proxied by discharge records from NVE's HydAPI for the station covering total outflow from the Arendalsvassdraget system (station 19.127.0, Rygene total), aggregated to ISO weeks. Electricity prices are day-ahead prices for the NO2 bidding zone, which covers this region. Initial-of-year reservoir filling is taken from NVE's Magasinstatistikk series for the NO2 zone, the finest publicly available resolution, rather than assumed at a fixed fraction. All data is real; no values are synthetic.

**Two models.** The first model (Phase A) aggregates the entire watercourse into a single reservoir with a system-level effective head, used as an initial, simplified test of the overall approach. The second model (Phase B), which this paper centers on, resolves three real plants individually in their true upstream-to-downstream order: Jorundland, Evenstad, and Rygene, each with its own real turbine capacity and head, and with Jorundland's release contributing to Evenstad's inflow and, in turn, to Rygene's. Jorundland is the only one of the three plants with a meaningful reservoir (167 GWh of working storage, identified from NVE's reservoir volume records for its headwater lake); Evenstad and Rygene are modeled as run-of-river.

**Policies.** Three release policies are compared for each backtest year. Open-loop (OL) commits a full 52-week release schedule at the start of the year using only information available at that time, represented as a stochastic linear program over a scenario tree with full non-anticipativity. Closed-loop (CL) is implemented as a rolling-horizon multistage stochastic program, re-solved quarterly as new information arrives, which is a tractable approximation to true SDDP rather than an implementation of it. Perfect foresight (PF) solves a deterministic linear program with the realized inflow and price path known in advance, and serves as an upper-bound benchmark rather than an achievable policy. All three are linear programs solved with HiGHS. The value of flexibility (VoF) for a given year is defined as CL revenue minus OL revenue.

**Forecasting.** Inflow and price scenario trees are generated from Bayesian seasonal autoregressive models fit independently for each backtest year using only data available before that year, with scenario trees reduced from posterior draws via k-means clustering. This out-of-sample design means the forecasting model calibrated for, say, 2022 has no knowledge of 2022's realized prices, which is central to the second result below.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Flexibility value concentrates at the storage node

Across all 22 backtest years and both routing-lag assumptions tested (same-week and one-week delay between plants), Evenstad and Rygene contribute exactly 0.000 MNOK of flexibility value in every single year. This is not a rounding artifact or a property of one particular policy implementation. It follows directly from the flow magnitudes in the data: Evenstad's local inflow alone, without any contribution from Jorundland, exceeds Evenstad's turbine capacity (3.95 GWh per week) in all 1,345 weeks of the 2000-2025 hydrological record, with a minimum observed local inflow of 6.46 GWh per week. Similarly, total system throughput at Rygene exceeds Rygene's turbine capacity (9.24 GWh per week) in all 1,345 weeks, with a minimum of 9.61 GWh per week. Both plants therefore generate at full capacity in every week regardless of what policy is used upstream; there is no scheduling decision left to make at either node, and consequently nothing for flexibility to be worth. This holds independently of whether either plant happens to have any reservoir storage of its own, because their local inflow alone already saturates their turbines.

Figure 1 (`results/figures/essay/fig1_vof_decomposition.png`) shows the per-plant decomposition of flexibility value across all 22 years. All visible variation is at Jorundland; Evenstad and Rygene are flat at zero throughout and are noted rather than plotted, since a line at exactly zero adds nothing to read. Averaged across all 22 years, Jorundland accounts for effectively 100 percent of the cascade's total flexibility value (mean VoF share of 0.9999999999999979, i.e. all of it to numerical precision).

### 4.2 Naive flexibility estimates become unreliable under regime shift, and the mechanism is traceable

The full 22-year sample includes the 2021-2022 European energy crisis, during which Norwegian electricity prices reached levels far outside the 2003-2020 distribution the year-by-year forecasting models were calibrated on. Restricting the backtest to the 17 years without an extreme price shock (annual mean price at or below 413 NOK/MWh, the 2003-2020 mean plus 1.5 standard deviations) versus the 5 years that exceed it (2018, 2021-2024) produces starkly different behavior, summarized in Table 1.

**Table 1. Value of flexibility at Jorundland, normal vs. crisis years (cascade model, same-week routing)**

| Group | n | Mean VoF_J (MNOK/yr) | Std. dev. | Wilcoxon p (CL > OL) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Normal years (annual mean price <= 413 NOK/MWh) | 17 | +3.40 | 5.98 | 0.013 |
| Crisis years (2018, 2021-2024) | 5 | -7.19 | 47.25 | n/a (too few years for a meaningful test) |

The standard deviation of the crisis-year estimates is nearly eight times that of the normal-year estimates, and the sign of the mean flips. This instability was checked against a competing explanation before accepting the regime-shift account: if Jorundland's reservoir were hitting its physical storage limits more often in crisis years, the OL and CL policies would diverge mechanically through binding constraints rather than through forecast failure. This is ruled out by the data: the backtest records zero storage-clamp events (weeks where the linear program is forced to a storage boundary) in all five crisis years, and the initial storage level at the start of each backtest year is statistically indistinguishable between the two groups (mean 113.5 GWh in both, against a 167 GWh capacity).

What does explain the instability is a measurable distributional mismatch. Figure 2a (`results/figures/essay/fig2_regime_shift_mechanism.png`) compares the realized weekly price pattern in 2022, normalized to its own annual mean, against the 2003-2020 seasonal climatology, also normalized. The two series are negatively correlated (r = -0.24): the calendar weeks that were historically cheap became, in 2022, some of the most expensive, and vice versa. Across all 22 years, the cross-sectional correlation between a year's annual mean price and its VoF_J estimate is r = -0.665 (p = 0.001), confirming that price-level extremity, not just seasonal-shape reversal, predicts estimate instability. Both the OL and CL policies are committing decisions against scenario trees that no longer describe the world that materializes; whichever policy happens to be better positioned for the particular timing of that year's price spikes outperforms the other by chance, not by skill.

A second, independent piece of evidence for the same mechanism comes from the routing-lag sensitivity analysis. Changing the assumed travel time between plants from zero weeks to one week should be a minor modeling detail in any well-behaved year, since it only shifts which calendar week a unit of water's downstream revenue is credited to. Figure 2b plots the absolute difference between the lag-0 and lag-1 VoF_J estimate against that year's annual mean price, across all 22 years. The correlation is r = 0.893 (p < 0.001): the more extreme the price year, the more this bookkeeping assumption swings the estimate, by as much as 71 MNOK in 2022 alone (lag-0: -79.7 MNOK, lag-1: -8.7 MNOK). This is what would be expected if the underlying signal is timing noise relative to an unanticipated price path rather than a real structural difference between routing assumptions: the same mechanism that destabilizes the OL-versus-CL comparison also destabilizes an unrelated modeling choice, in proportion to that year's price extremity.

The same qualitative pattern, large and unstable flexibility estimates that flip sign during 2021-2022, also appears independently in the single-reservoir Phase A model built earlier in this project. We do not claim the magnitude of this effect scales precisely between the two models; the two models differ in total system size, head, and the degree to which storage is aggregated across plants, all of which affect the absolute scale of revenue and VoF on their own. What we do observe is that the same qualitative breakdown, sign instability concentrated in the crisis years, appears at both levels of model granularity, which is evidence that the underlying mechanism is a property of forecasting against an out-of-distribution market regime, not an artifact specific to one model's structure.

## 5. Discussion

The practical implication of these two results, taken together, is that a single annual or multi-year average value-of-flexibility number, computed without checking whether the realized period included a market regime shift, can be actively misleading rather than merely noisy. The full 22-year average at Jorundland (+0.99 MNOK/yr) is close in sign to the 17-year normal-year average, but only because the crisis years happen to average out near zero in this particular sample; the underlying year-by-year behavior in those years is large in magnitude and unstable in sign, not small. Anyone using a simplified flexibility estimate, for investment appraisal, regulatory cost-benefit analysis, or operational tooling, should check whether the result is being driven by a small number of out-of-distribution years before treating an average as representative.

The concentration result has a related implication for cascade systems more broadly: searching for flexibility value at a run-of-river plant is not merely unlikely to find much, it is structurally guaranteed to find exactly zero whenever local inflow exceeds turbine capacity, a condition checkable directly from historical flow data without running any optimization at all. Computational effort in valuing flexibility across a real cascade is best directed entirely at the nodes with storage.

Several limitations bound these conclusions. The closed-loop policy is a rolling-horizon multistage approximation, not full SDDP, so all flexibility-value estimates here should be read as lower bounds on what a full SDDP implementation would recover. The routing assumption between plants uses a simplified same-week or one-week lag rather than measured hydraulic travel time, which was not available from public sources; this does not change the headline result in normal years, but its interaction with extreme price shocks should be treated as a modeling sensitivity rather than ground truth. The single-reservoir Phase A model used for the cross-model consistency check in Section 4.2 aggregates substantially more total storage than the cascade model represents on this flow path, so the absolute VoF magnitudes between the two models are not directly comparable; only the qualitative pattern of crisis-driven instability is claimed as consistent across them. The Magasinstatistikk-derived initial storage values are at NO2 zone resolution, the finest publicly available, rather than specific to the Arendalsvassdraget watercourse, introducing some noise into each year's starting condition.

A natural next step, not undertaken here, is to re-run the closed-loop policy with true SDDP and check whether the normal-year flexibility premium grows once the policy can fully exploit recourse, and whether crisis-year instability is reduced by a better-specified terminal value function.
